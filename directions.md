### Option A — PocketSod hosts (recommended)
- Site lives at `clientname.pocketsod.com` on GitHub Pages, managed by PocketSod
- Include first year of hosting in the package price ($500–$1,000)
- Upsell hosting management at $20–$50/mo after year one
- Pro: simpler for the client, creates recurring revenue
- Con: you're responsible if it goes down; client is tied to you

### Option B — Deliver files, client hosts
- Zip and hand off HTML/CSS/JS; help client deploy to their own GitHub Pages or Netlify
- Pro: clean handoff, no ongoing dependency
- Con: harder to upsell maintenance; client may break it

**Most freelance designers at this tier use Option A** — host under your own subdomain, first year free, then charge the monthly add-on.

## Freelance Web Design Best Practices

### 1. Always Use a Contract
Never start work without a signed agreement. This protects both you and the client.

A basic contract should cover:
- **Scope of work** — exactly what you will build (pages, features, revisions)
- **What is NOT included** — just as important; prevents scope creep
- **Payment terms** — amounts, due dates, method
- **Revision policy** — how many rounds, what counts as a revision
- **Timeline** — start date, milestone dates, final delivery date
- **Ownership** — who owns the site and code after payment is complete
- **Kill fee** — what happens if the client cancels mid-project (e.g., you keep the deposit)
- **Content responsibility** — client is responsible for providing text and images by a deadline

Free contract templates to start from: Hello Bonsai, AND.CO, or the Contract Killer (open source).

---

### 2. Payment Terms — Never Work for Free Upfront

Standard freelance practice:
- **50% deposit before work begins** — non-refundable. This filters out tire-kickers and covers your time if the client disappears.
- **50% on final delivery** — paid before you hand over files or point the domain.

For larger projects (Growth/Premium), you can split into thirds: 33% to start, 33% at mid-point (design approval), 33% on launch.

Never deliver the final site or hand over credentials until the final payment clears.

---

### 3. Scope Creep — Protect Your Time

Scope creep is when a client keeps adding requests beyond what was agreed. It is the #1 reason freelancers lose money.

How to prevent it:
- Define scope in the contract with specific page names and feature list
- When a client requests something outside scope, say: *"That's outside our current agreement — I can add it for $X, or we can swap it for something else on the list."*
- Put change requests in writing (email is fine) before doing the work
- Never say yes verbally and assume you'll sort it out later

---

### 4. Revision Policy

One revision round means:
- You deliver the site
- Client gives **consolidated** feedback in one batch (not drip-fed over days)
- You implement it once
- Done

Make this explicit in your contract and repeat it at delivery. Clients who give feedback piece by piece will consume your time.

If a client wants more than one revision round, charge for it. A second round at the Starter tier might be $100–$200.

---

### 5. Client Communication

- **Use email for everything important** — decisions, approvals, change requests. Texts and calls are fine for conversation but always follow up in writing: *"Just to confirm what we discussed — you approved the blue color scheme and want the contact form to go to your Gmail."*
- **Set response time expectations** — tell the client upfront: *"I respond within 1 business day, Monday–Friday."*
- **Set content deadlines** — if the client hasn't sent their logo and text by a certain date, the project timeline shifts. Put this in the contract.
- **Weekly check-in** — a short email update ("here's where we are, here's what I need from you") keeps clients calm and reduces anxious follow-ups.

---

### 6. Content Collection

Most delays in web projects are caused by waiting on the client for content (text, photos, logo).

Best practice:
- Give the client a **content checklist** at kickoff (your build guides already have this)
- Set a hard deadline: *"I need all content by [date] to meet your launch date. If content arrives late, the timeline shifts accordingly."*
- If they don't have a logo, either charge to design one or use a text-based placeholder and note it in the contract
- If they don't have photos, point them to free stock sites (Unsplash, Pexels) or charge for sourcing

---

### 7. Discovery Call Before Quoting

Before sending a proposal, do a 20–30 minute discovery call to:
- Understand their business and goals
- Find out if they have a domain, logo, and existing content
- Learn their timeline and budget range
- Identify red flags (indecisive, unrealistic expectations, "I just want something simple" with a list of 20 features)

This call helps you scope accurately and filter out clients who aren't a fit. Don't skip it even for Starter-tier projects.

---

### 8. Onboarding After They Say Yes

Once a client agrees to hire you:
1. Send the contract (use an e-signature tool like DocuSign, HelloSign, or even a PDF they sign and email back)
2. Send the invoice for the 50% deposit
3. Send the content intake form/checklist
4. Only begin work after the contract is signed AND the deposit is paid

Do not begin work on goodwill. Clients who won't sign a contract or pay a deposit are red flags.

---

### 9. Delivery & Handoff

At final delivery, provide the client with:
- **Live site URL** (or file package if they're hosting)
- **Login credentials** for anything they own (domain registrar, hosting, form backend)
- A **brief written summary** of how to make simple updates (e.g., how to change their phone number, how to update business hours)
- Any ongoing service details (if they're on a maintenance plan)

Keep a copy of all credentials in a secure password manager. You'll need them for maintenance work.

---

### 10. Ongoing Relationship & Recurring Revenue

A delivered site is not the end — it's the start of a recurring revenue opportunity.

- Offer a **monthly maintenance plan** at delivery ($50–$200/mo depending on scope)
- Maintenance can include: plugin/software updates, content edits, uptime monitoring, security checks, annual hosting renewal
- Even clients who don't want maintenance will sometimes come back for updates — charge hourly ($75–$150/hr is standard for small-business web work)
- Ask for a **referral** at the end of every successful project: *"If you know anyone else who needs a website, I'd love the introduction."*

---

### 11. Pricing Confidence

- Don't undercharge trying to win the job. Clients who haggle your price down will also demand the most work.
- Your Starter package at $500–$1,000 is already at the low end of the market. A basic 3-page site from a professional agency costs $2,000–$5,000.
- If a client says "that's too expensive," it usually means they don't yet see the value — explain what they're getting, don't immediately drop your price.
- It's okay to turn down clients who aren't a fit. One bad client can consume the time of three good ones.

---

### 12. Taxes & Business Finance (basics)

- Keep business income and expenses in a separate bank account from day one
- Set aside **25–30% of every payment** for taxes (self-employment tax + income tax)
- Track every expense — software, subscriptions, equipment, home office — these are deductible
- Use simple invoicing software (Wave is free; QuickBooks Simple Start is ~$15/mo)
- Talk to a CPA before your first tax filing as a self-employed person

---

### 13. Red Flag Clients to Avoid

Walk away from clients who:
- Won't sign a contract or pay a deposit
- Say "it'll only take you 5 minutes" repeatedly
- Have an extremely vague brief and resist answering intake questions
- Say a previous developer "ripped them off" or "did terrible work" with no specifics
- Want a complete site for $100–$200
- Need it done "by tomorrow"
- Contact you at all hours and expect immediate responses

One difficult client will teach you to trust this list.
